Do I Actually Need an ERP?
A Guide for Small Food Distributors

FreeBeginner ⏱ 9 min read
◆ ERP FOR FOOD DISTRIBUTORS     ◆ WINMAN ERP     ◆ AIRTABLE ALTERNATIVE     ◆ FOOD BUSINESS SYSTEMS     ◆ ERP FOR FOOD DISTRIBUTORS     ◆ WINMAN ERP     ◆ AIRTABLE ALTERNATIVE     ◆ FOOD BUSINESS SYSTEMS    

Every few months a small food distribution business asks us some version of this: "We're outgrowing our spreadsheets. Do we need an ERP?" The honest answer is usually: not yet — but here's how to know when you do.

ERP systems are powerful. They're also expensive, complex to implement, and often overkill for businesses under a certain size. Buying one too early can be just as painful as waiting too long.

The short version: If you're under 20 staff and processing fewer than 100 orders a week, a well-configured Airtable setup with smart integrations will almost certainly serve you better than an ERP — at a fraction of the cost. Read on for the full picture.

What an ERP actually does

ERP stands for Enterprise Resource Planning. In a food distribution context, it typically handles:

  • Purchase orders, sales orders, and order fulfilment
  • Stock management and warehouse operations
  • Financial accounting (invoicing, payments, P&L)
  • Production planning (for manufacturers)
  • Customer and supplier relationship data
  • Reporting and business intelligence

The key word is integrated. An ERP does all of this in one system, with data flowing between modules automatically. That's genuinely powerful — when your business is complex enough to need it.

What to use instead of an ERP (for now)

For most small food distributors and wholesalers, the right stack looks something like this:

  • Airtable — as your product catalogue (PIM), inventory tracker, and order log. Structured, API-connected, and your team can use it without training.
  • Shopify — if you sell online, handling your eCommerce orders, product listings, and customer accounts.
  • Make.com — to connect everything. Order confirmation emails, stock updates, supplier notifications — automated without code.
  • Xero or QuickBooks — for your accounting. Both have good APIs and integrate with Make.com.
  • AI email-to-order workflows — if you receive orders by email, AI can handle the reading and draft order creation automatically.

This stack costs a few hundred pounds a month to run, takes weeks to implement rather than months, and your team can manage it themselves. When you outgrow it — and you'll know when you do — the structured data you've built in Airtable makes migration to an ERP significantly easier.

Which ERP for food businesses?

When the time does come, the most common options for UK food distributors and manufacturers are:

  • Winman — built for UK manufacturers and distributors, strong on stock control and production. Good REST API and SFTP capabilities for integration.
  • Sage 200 — widely used in UK SMEs, strong accounting module, integrates well with many food industry add-ons.
  • Microsoft Dynamics 365 Business Central — deeper Microsoft integration, good for businesses already running heavily on M365.
