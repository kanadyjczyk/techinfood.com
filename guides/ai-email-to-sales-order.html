AI Email to Sales Order:
How It Works for Food Businesses

Free guideBeginner ⏱ 8 min read
◆ AI EMAIL ORDER AUTOMATION     ◆ SALES ORDER PROCESSING     ◆ FOOD DISTRIBUTOR TECH     ◆ NO MORE RE-KEYING     ◆ AI EMAIL ORDER AUTOMATION     ◆ SALES ORDER PROCESSING     ◆ FOOD DISTRIBUTOR TECH     ◆ NO MORE RE-KEYING    

If you run a food distribution or wholesale business, you probably spend a significant chunk of every day reading order emails and manually entering them into your system. A customer emails "can we get 20 oat milks and a case of sourdough for Friday" — and someone on your team has to find the right SKUs, quantities, and delivery date, then key it into whatever system you use.

Multiply that by 30 orders a day and you've got hours of avoidable admin. This guide explains exactly how AI fixes that — and what you need to get started.

How it works — step by step

📥
Customer sends an order email
Any format — a paragraph, a bullet list, a reply to a previous email, a forwarded spreadsheet, or even a voice note transcript. The AI handles all of them.
✦
AI reads and extracts the order
Natural language processing identifies: product references, quantities, any delivery date or address, and special instructions. It works even when customers write informally or use their own names for your products.
✦ AI step
✦
Lines matched to your product catalogue
Fuzzy matching compares what the customer wrote against your SKU list. "oat milk" matches "Organic Oat Milk 1L (OOM-001)". Confidence scores flag anything uncertain for human review.
✦ AI step
✅
Draft sales order created
A complete draft order is generated in your system — with customer, products, quantities, delivery date, and pricing. You review it, make any changes, and confirm with one click.
📤
Confirmation sent automatically
Once confirmed, an order confirmation email goes back to the customer — with your branding, order reference, and delivery details. No one on your team needs to write it.

What you need to implement this

You don't need to build AI from scratch. Here's the stack most small food businesses use:

  • Make.com — watches your inbox and orchestrates the workflow. Free plan available; paid plans from £9/month.
  • An LLM API — OpenAI (GPT-4), Anthropic (Claude), or Google (Gemini) to do the reading and extraction. Costs pennies per email at typical food business volumes.
  • Your product catalogue in a structured format — Airtable works well. The AI compares against this to match product names.
  • Your order management system or ERP — where the draft order lands. Airtable, Winman, or a simple Google Sheet all work depending on your setup.
Is this hard to set up? The Make.com orchestration layer is manageable without code. The LLM prompt engineering — writing clear instructions so the AI extracts data reliably — is the part most people want help with.

When does this make sense?

This workflow is worth implementing if:

  • You receive 10 or more order emails per day
  • Your customers order in varied or informal formats
  • Someone on your team spends meaningful time re-keying orders
  • You have a structured product catalogue to match against (or are willing to build one)

If you receive 2–3 orders a day in a consistent format, a simpler email template approach may be enough first.
